## 7. Questions to Ask the Broker / Landlord

1. Is there a broker fee, and exactly how much? What is the total upfront cost (first month, security, fee, any admin)?
2. Is this unit rent-stabilized or market-rate/deregulated? What was the prior legal registered rent?
3. Why did the last tenant leave, and how long did they live here?
4. Are the listing photos of **this exact unit (3A)**, or a model?
5. What is the lease term, and what have renewal increases looked like?
6. **Heat and hot water:** were there outages last winter? (The building has multiple "entire building" heat complaints on record.)
7. Is there any history of mice, roaches, or bedbugs in this line of units, and what's the extermination schedule?
8. Any mold or leak history in 3A (there's an open roof-leak violation in 4C)?
9. What utilities are included vs. tenant-paid (heat, hot water, cooking gas, electric)?
10. Is there A/C, or would I install window units?
11. What are the income and guarantor requirements (typically 40x rent / guarantor 80x)?
12. Application timeline and what documents are needed?
13. Any planned construction or repairs in the building?

## 8. Negotiation Recommendation

**Negotiate — yes, but modestly, and lead with concessions over price.** The rent is already at/below neighborhood median, so a big rent cut is unlikely. Leverage comes from the **building's documented HPD/311 problems** and the fact that **multiple identical units are simultaneously available**, giving the landlord competition against himself.

- **Target rent:** ask for **$1,900** (a 5% trim) or hold at $1,950.
- **Better ask — concessions:** **waive or halve the broker fee**, or **one month free** on a 13-month lease (effective ~$1,850/mo), plus a **written commitment to resolve the open Class C violations and confirm heat reliability** before move-in.
- **Also negotiable:** flexible start date, fresh paint, professional extermination before move-in, and a working A/C allowance.
- **Leverage:** open hazardous violations + winter heat complaints + several vacant sister units.
- **Risk of negotiating:** low — with this much building inventory, pushing back rarely loses you the unit; the bigger risk is the building itself, not the negotiation.

**Short message you can send the broker:**

> Hi — I'm interested in 1974 51st St #3A at $2,000. Before I apply, could you confirm: (1) the exact broker fee and total move-in cost, (2) whether it's rent-stabilized and the prior legal rent, and (3) the lease term? I also noticed the building has some open HPD violations and prior heat complaints, so I'd want written confirmation that heat/hot water are reliable and any open items in the unit are resolved before move-in. Given a few similar units are available, would you consider $1,900 or a waived/reduced broker fee? I can move quickly for the right terms. Thanks!

## 9. Final Verdict

### Tour only if negotiable

The rent is reasonable and the F train is genuinely close, but the building carries **high, recent, recurring maintenance risk** (heat outages, pests, mold, 15 open Class C violations) under an owner with a heavy eviction and deregulation record. Only proceed if you can tour the actual unit, confirm heat reliability, and secure a fee/rent concession.

**Top 3 pros**
1. Genuinely close, all-hours F train (~2–3 min walk) and dense retail on 20th Ave.
2. Rent at/below the Borough Park 1BR median, with real negotiation room.
3. Safe, residential neighborhood (66th Precinct) with a live-in super and courtyard.

**Top 3 cons**
1. High building risk: 15 open Class C hazards, chronic winter heat/hot-water outages, active pest and mold complaints (2025–2026).
2. Aggressive landlord profile: 27 eviction filings and stabilized units cut 47 → 14.
3. Walk-up, no A/C, no in-unit laundry, undisclosed fee, and reused/generic listing photos.

**Most likely reason units here cycle:** market-rate repricing and high turnover on deregulated units, compounded by maintenance issues that push tenants out — not a stale-listing problem for 3A specifically (it's brand-new on market).

**Plain-English advice:** The price and the train are good, but the building's paper trail is a warning sign — this is a landlord who churns units and has repeated heat, pest, and mold problems on record. Don't apply sight-unseen. Tour the actual apartment (ideally on a cold evening), get the fee and heat answers in writing, and only sign if they knock off the broker fee or a month of rent and commit to fixing the open violations. If they won't budge or won't confirm heat reliability, walk — there are cheaper, cleaner 1BRs nearby.
